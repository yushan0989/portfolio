Project Description & Objective

Developed a data-driven consumer behavior analysis for a strategic retail partner, utilizing a large-scale electronic invoice dataset to decode the drivers of consumer demand. The primary objective was to move beyond raw sales figures by establishing a robust correlation between meteorological fluctuations, such as temperature and precipitation, and the consumption patterns of specific convenience store products, such as Japanese Oden. By quantifying how weather sensitivity varies by region, the project gave the retailer practical insights to improve inventory management and demand forecasting.

Methods & Data

The analysis utilized a comprehensive dataset of over 9.2 million electronic invoice records from 2018. To ensure the reliability of the findings, I first conducted a sampling validation by comparing the geographic distribution of the data against official Ministry of Finance statistics, confirming the dataset‚Äôs representative nature across Taiwan.

After identifying and filtering out anomalous high-frequency users (e.g., single-day consumption exceeding 60 transactions), I segmented the data to isolate weather-sensitive products. I employed Two Sample t-tests and Regression to analyze the impact of environmental variables. To account for regional psychological thresholds, I redefined "low temperature" using a 20th percentile threshold specific to each city (19¬∞C for high-latitude city and 23¬∞C for low-latitude city), ensuring the analysis reflected local consumer perceptions of "cold" weather.

Key Deliverables

Figure 1 demonstrates the initial validation phase, ensuring the representativeness of the sample. Figure 2, a regression analysis, establishes a significant negative correlation between observed temperature and daily sales volume, illustrating a marked non-linear increase in demand as temperatures drop. With a statistical significance of p < 0.001, these findings confirm temperature as a primary driver of sales volatility, providing a robust empirical foundation for optimizing inventory and retail demand forecasting.

Figure 3 utilizes a regional 20th percentile threshold of 19¬∞C to identify a significant surge in sales volume during low-temperature events. Impact & Insights

This project highlighted a critical nuance in retail analytics: consumer perception is relative. By discovering that a 50% sales surge in both high-latitude and low-latitude cities was triggered by different temperature thresholds, I demonstrated that localized environmental benchmarks are more effective than universal ones for national supply chain planning.

Furthermore, the analysis successfully differentiated between "weather-sensitive" and "weather-neutral" products (such as pudding and cold noodles), allowing the client to prioritize which SKUs require weather-contingent marketing. This experience refined my ability to translate abstract statistical p-values into concrete commercial strategies, bridging the gap between data science and retail operations.
