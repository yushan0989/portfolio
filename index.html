Graduate Thesis

Macroeconomic and Cross-Sectoral Impacts of ETS2: A Scenario Analysis for the Netherlands

The Approach

Developed a robust Scenario Analysis framework using the Applied General Equilibrium (AGE) model in GAMS. Simulated distinct policy pathways to analyze cross-sectoral impacts on the Dutch economy using Statistics Netherlands data.

The Model Structure

The structure simplifies complex intersectoral linkages by aggregating primary inputs into Labour and Capital. Sectoral production follows a Cobbâ€“Douglas structure, characterized by constant returns to scale and diminishing marginal productivity, ensuring a robust foundation for evaluating resource reallocation under environmental constraints. Environmental impacts are integrated by treating CO2 emissions as by-products of production, where each sector is assigned a fixed emission coefficient. This allows the model to calculate total emissions as a product of sectoral output. Critically, the carbon price is treated as an endogenous shadow price of the emissions cap; it reflects the marginal abatement cost when the cap is binding, and remains zero otherwise. This methodology effectively captures direct environmental impacts while preserving the analytical clarity needed for strategic climate policy assessment.

The Result

The implementation of ETS2 in the Netherlands triggers a significant shift of labor and capital away from high-emission sectors, such as road transport and buildings. As carbon prices internalize environmental costs, the model captures a distinct transition toward lower-carbon activities. This demonstrates the systemâ€™s effectiveness in driving sectoral transformation, while highlighting the need for industries to adapt to shifting relative prices and resource availability.
The analysis identified a critical non-linear relationship between emission targets and economic impacts. While modest reductions (up to 15%) can be achieved with manageable welfare costs, deeper decarbonization targets (reaching 37%) lead to a sharp, exponential increase in marginal abatement costs. This analysis provides a data-driven roadmap for policy-makers to balance environmental ambition with economic stability.
